\begin{abstract}
Coherent diffractive imaging and ptychography recover structure from
intensity-only diffraction measurements, coupling global wave physics with sharp
local object features. We introduce SRU-Net, a spectral-residual U-Net that
combines Fourier-style global mixing with convolutional local recovery inside a
physics-informed reconstruction loop. On synthetic line-pattern CDI, hybrid
spectral-convolutional rows improve reconstruction quality over CNN, FNO, and
FFNO alternatives under a matched protocol. On PDEBench CNS, a secondary
supervised benchmark run with a reduced trajectory split, FFNO is the
lowest-error row while SRU-Net$^*$ remains ahead of FNO and U-Net baselines,
supporting the broader global-local architecture thesis.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Coherent diffractive imaging (CDI) and ptychography image specimens by measuring
far-field diffraction intensities rather than by forming images with lenses.
This removes optical resolution limits but creates a nonlinear phase-retrieval
problem: detectors record the squared magnitude of a diffracted wave, while the
phase needed for direct inversion is lost. Classical iterative methods such as
alternating real- and Fourier-space projections can recover high-quality
reconstructions, but they are often too slow for high-throughput microscopy,
\emph{in situ} experiments, and streaming beamline operation.

Physics-informed deep reconstruction addresses this bottleneck by combining the
speed of neural inference with a differentiable physical forward model. The
PtychoPINN framework of Hoidn, Mishra, and Mehta demonstrated that an
unsupervised ptychographic network can be trained by composing a neural
generator with real-space overlap constraints and a far-field diffraction map,
then minimizing measurement-space error under photon-counting statistics
\cite{hoidn2023physics}. That work establishes the training principle used here:
the network is not merely asked to match labels, but is constrained to produce
reconstructions whose forward diffraction is physically consistent with the
measurements.

This leaves an architectural question. Ptychographic inversion combines two
different demands. The diffraction measurement encodes global wave interactions
and phase coupling, while the target object contains local features such as
edges, phase wraps, and fine texture. Convolutional encoder-decoder models are
strong local image priors, but their long-range interaction capacity grows
indirectly through depth and pooling. Fourier neural operators provide global
mixing in a single layer, but spectral truncation can suppress the high-frequency
local structure that often determines reconstruction quality.

We therefore study SRU-Net, a spectral-residual U-Net. The
model uses Fourier-style spectral mixing for global context, convolutional paths
for local structure, and a U-shaped residual encoder-decoder for image-domain
refinement. The central hypothesis is not that Fourier layers should replace
convolutions, but that a generator for scientific imaging should explicitly
combine global operator mixing with local feature preservation.

We evaluate this hypothesis in two settings. The primary setting is
physics-informed ptychographic reconstruction on a 128-by-128 synthetic
line-pattern CDI benchmark. The secondary setting is PDEBench 2D compressible
Navier-Stokes (CNS), which tests the same global-local inductive bias in
time-dependent field prediction. CNS is a stress test because it mixes
coherent large-scale dynamics with
localized shocks, vortices, and high-gradient features, and because purely
spectral operator models can struggle when localized structure dominates
predictive accuracy. The CNS benchmark therefore tests whether the same
global-local inductive bias transfers to a different scientific domain.

The contributions are:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item a spectral-local residual generator for physics-informed
  ptychographic reconstruction;
  \item a shared mathematical description of the architecture across CDI
  reconstruction and CNS forward prediction; and
  \item a two-part experimental program: primary synthetic line-pattern CDI
  reconstruction and secondary PDEBench CNS prediction.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work}

\subsection{Physics-informed ptychographic reconstruction}

Hoidn, Mishra, and Mehta introduced PtychoPINN as a physics-constrained,
unsupervised deep learning method for scanning coherent diffraction
reconstruction \cite{hoidn2023physics}. Their approach composes a neural
generator with a real-space consistency operator derived from overlapping scan
measurements and a far-field diffraction forward model. The training loss is
applied after forward mapping the reconstruction back into the measurement
domain, and the method incorporates photon-counting statistics through a
probabilistic loss. This establishes the key prior for the present work:
physical consistency can be enforced through the reconstruction loop without
requiring paired ground-truth labels for every experimental measurement.

The present work keeps that conceptual division between learned generator and
known physics. Our focus is the generator architecture. Rather than treating the
reconstruction network as a generic CNN image prior, we ask whether the network
should encode the same global-local structure as the inverse problem itself.

\subsection{Fourier neural operators and spectral mixing}

Fourier neural operators (FNOs) learn mappings between functions by applying
global spectral convolutions in Fourier space~\cite{fno}. This gives every
spectral layer a global receptive field and is well matched to wave-like and
PDE-governed systems. Standard FNO layers also include a local transform added
to the spectral path, usually a pointwise channel mixing term. This
spectral-plus-local form is conceptually attractive for diffraction and flow
problems, where long-range coherent structure and local features coexist.

However, FNO-style truncation of Fourier modes can bias the model toward
low-frequency structure. In imaging and compressible flow, local structure such
as edges, speckle-like texture, shocks, vortices, and turbulent features can be
central to the task. This motivates architectures that retain spectral global
mixing while adding local paths and decoder-side refinement.

\subsection{Spectral-convolutional architectures}

Recent neural-operator variants and hybrid models combine Fourier blocks with
CNN or U-Net structure. U-FNO-style architectures add U-Net multiscale structure
around Fourier layers~\cite{ufno}. Localized-kernel neural operators augment
global spectral layers with stronger local kernels~\cite{localno}.
FourierUnet-like architectures place spectral blocks inside U-shaped image
models. These designs all reflect the same principle: spectral layers are useful
for global coupling, but local convolutions and skip connections remain
important for high-frequency details.

The architecture studied here is closest in spirit to an FNO encoder embedded
in a U-shaped image model. The implemented SRU-Net body combines
spectral-local encoding, a ResNet bottleneck, and learned upsampling. This combination preserves the global
mixing benefits of neural operators while retaining the local image prior that
made CNN-based PtychoPINN effective.

\section{Methods}

\subsection{Task-agnostic generator formulation}

Let $x$ denote the task input and $y$ the target field or reconstruction. We
write the learned generator as
\begin{equation}
  \hat{y} = G_{\theta}(x).
\end{equation}
For CDI, $x$ is a stack of diffraction measurements and $\hat{y}$ is a complex
object-patch representation. For PDEBench CNS, $x$ is a temporal history of
physical fields and $\hat{y}$ is the predicted next state. The tasks differ in
their adapters and losses, but share the same architectural principle:
$G_{\theta}$ combines global spectral mixing with local convolutional feature
recovery.

We decompose the generator as
\begin{equation}
  G_{\theta} = D_{\theta} \circ B_{\theta} \circ E_{\theta},
\end{equation}
where $E_{\theta}$ is the spectral-local encoder, $B_{\theta}$ is a ResNet
bottleneck at reduced spatial resolution, and $D_{\theta}$ is a convolutional
upsampling decoder.

\subsection{Spectral-local encoding}

For a feature tensor $z_{\ell}$, a spectral-local encoder block has the form
\begin{equation}
z_{\ell+1}
= \sigma\left(
L_{\ell} z_{\ell}
+ \mathcal{F}^{-1}
\left[
R_{\ell}(k)\,\mathcal{T}_{K}\left(\mathcal{F}(z_{\ell})\right)(k)
\right]
\right),
\end{equation}
where $L_{\ell}$ is a learned local convolutional map, $\mathcal{F}$ is the 2D
Fourier transform, $\mathcal{T}_{K}$ retains a fixed set of Fourier modes,
$R_{\ell}(k)$ is a learned complex spectral mixing tensor over retained modes,
and $\sigma$ is a pointwise nonlinearity. In residual form, this can be written
as
\begin{equation}
z_{\ell+1}
= z_{\ell} + \sigma\left(L_{\ell} z_{\ell} + S_{\ell} z_{\ell}\right),
\end{equation}
with $S_{\ell}$ denoting the truncated spectral operator. The local path
preserves high-frequency and edge-like information that may be weakened by
spectral truncation, while the spectral path provides global coupling.

upsampling decoder. Figure~\ref{fig:srunet_architecture} summarizes the data flow, and

When skip connections are enabled, encoder features $e_s$ are fused into decoder
states $d_s$. For additive fusion,
\begin{equation}
  d_s = U_s(d_{s+1}) + P_s(e_s),
\end{equation}
where $U_s$ upsamples the coarser decoder state and $P_s$ projects the encoder
feature into the decoder channel space. For concatenative fusion,
\begin{equation}
  d_s = P_s\left([U_s(d_{s+1}), e_s]\right).
\end{equation}
The CDI generator outputs complex object patches in real/imaginary format. The
PDEBench supervised adapter reuses the generator body but replaces the
ptychographic input and output representation with ordinary real-valued field
tensors.

\subsection{Physics-informed CDI training}

For ptychographic CDI, the generator predicts local complex object patches,
\begin{equation}
  \hat{o} = G_{\theta}(x).
\end{equation}
The prediction is passed through a real-space consistency operator $F_c$, which
stitches overlapping local patches into a common object canvas and re-extracts
position-consistent patches. The consistent object patches are then passed
through a known diffraction map $F_d$:
\begin{equation}
  \hat{x}
  = F_d\left(F_c(\hat{o}; r); P\right),
\end{equation}
where $r$ denotes scan positions and $P$ is the probe. The CDI objective is
defined in measurement space:
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{CDI}}
  = \mathcal{D}\left(x, F_d(F_c(G_{\theta}(x); r); P)\right)
  + \lambda R(G_{\theta}(x)).
\end{equation}
Here $\mathcal{D}$ is the experiment-specific measurement loss, such as Poisson
negative log likelihood or amplitude/intensity reconstruction error, and $R$
denotes optional reconstruction regularization. This preserves the
physics-informed training principle: the generator is optimized through the
forward diffraction model rather than only through direct image-space labels.

\subsection{PDEBench CNS supervised adapter}

For the CNS benchmark, the same architectural body is evaluated as a supervised
forward predictor. Let
\begin{equation}
  u_t = [\rho_t, v_{x,t}, v_{y,t}, p_t]
\end{equation}
be the density, velocity, and pressure state. Given a history length $h$, the
input is
\begin{equation}
  x_t = [u_{t-h}, \ldots, u_{t-1}],
\end{equation}
and the model predicts
\begin{equation}
  \hat{u}_t = G_{\theta}(x_t).
\end{equation}
The training objective is mean-squared error on normalized physical fields:
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{CNS}}
  = \left\|\hat{u}_t - u_t\right\|_2^2.
\end{equation}
Evaluation is performed after denormalization and reports RMSE, nRMSE, relative
$L_2$, and Fourier-band RMSE over low, mid, and high radial-frequency bands. The
high-frequency metric is included because shocks and localized vortical features
are precisely where global spectral models may lose detail.

\section{Experiments}

\subsection{Primary CDI benchmark: synthetic line-pattern ptychography}

The primary benchmark is a 128-by-128 synthetic line-pattern ptychographic
reconstruction task designed to stress local edge recovery in a controlled phase-retrieval
setting. Each sample is generated from sharp line-like objects, a known probe,
and simulated far-field diffraction measurements. This benchmark is the direct
test of the physics-informed reconstruction model: the network predicts complex
object patches, the ptychographic consistency and diffraction operators map the
prediction back to detector space, and reconstruction quality is measured
against held-out object amplitude and phase.

The CDI comparison uses $N=128$ object grids, two training objects, two
held-out test objects, $10^9$ photons per exposure, 40 training epochs,
learning rate $2\times 10^{-4}$, ReduceLROnPlateau scheduling,
and complex-valued real/imaginary generator output.

CDI results are reported by model and training procedure: SRU-Net + PINN,
FFNO + PINN, FNO + PINN, and CNN + PINN denote architectures trained through
the ptychographic diffraction forward model rather than through direct
supervised image labels. Rows without ``+ PINN'' denote non-PINN baselines.

\subsection{Secondary cross-domain benchmark: PDEBench CNS}

The secondary benchmark is PDEBench 2D compressible Navier-Stokes at native
$128\times128$ resolution. It stresses the same architectural tradeoff in a
different physical setting: coherent global flow and wave structure must be
predicted while preserving localized shocks, vortices, and high-gradient field
features. It therefore tests whether the SRU-Net architecture
transfers beyond the CDI inverse problem.

The selected CNS data are the official 128-by-128 periodic compressible-flow
training trajectories at Mach number $M=1.0$, shear viscosity
$\eta=0.01$, and bulk viscosity $\zeta=0.01$. The spatial grid has
$\Delta x=\Delta y=1/128=0.0078125$, the temporal spacing is
$\Delta t=0.05$, and the boundary condition is periodic. Each trajectory
contains the fields density, $v_x$, $v_y$, and pressure on a $128\times128$
grid. Splits are trajectory-level, normalization is computed from the train
split only, and metrics are evaluated on denormalized predictions.

\section{Discussion}

The core architectural claim is that physics-informed scientific reconstruction
benefits from separating two roles that are often conflated. The task physics
should constrain the output through a differentiable forward model or a
well-defined supervised field-prediction task. The generator should then be
chosen for the structure of the inverse or predictive mapping. For ptychography,
that mapping is both global and local: diffraction couples the object through
Fourier-domain wave physics, while the object itself contains sharp local
features. For compressible flow, large-scale coherent dynamics coexist with
localized shocks and vortices.

The SRU-Net encodes this global-local structure directly. Fourier-style
encoder blocks provide global mixing; local convolutional branches and decoder
features preserve high-frequency information; the ResNet bottleneck gives the
model capacity to refine features at reduced spatial resolution. This differs
from both pure CNN reconstruction and pure spectral operator modeling.

The evidence also argues for a narrow interpretation. The primary CDI result
compares local CNN, FNO, FFNO, SRU-Net, and spectral SRU-Net rows under one
synthetic line-pattern protocol, while the supervised FFNO control isolates one
training-procedure contrast. The CNS results use a reduced trajectory split as
secondary cross-domain architecture evidence. They test
architectural transfer under different physics, not whether the CDI forward
model applies to fluids. The SRU-Net$^*$ notation marks the CNS
spectral-bottleneck row.
